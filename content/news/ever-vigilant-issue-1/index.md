## Security Leadership

### Enhancing Security with Password Vaults

By {{< contributor author="Warren McComb" >}}

In today's digital landscape, maintaining robust security practices is paramount for safeguarding sensitive information. Password management stands at the forefront of these efforts, and one invaluable tool in this regard is the password vault. Password vaults serve as secure repositories for storing and managing passwords, offering numerous benefits for individuals and organizations alike.

One of the primary advantages of password vaults is their ability to generate strong, complex passwords. Unlike easily guessable passwords such as "123456" or "password," which remain prevalent despite their inherent risks, password vaults can generate randomized strings of characters that are virtually impossible for attackers to predict. This significantly strengthens the security posture of users and helps mitigate the risk of unauthorized access to accounts.

Moreover, password vaults facilitate the practice of using unique passwords for each account. Reusing passwords across multiple accounts is a common pitfall that can have disastrous consequences if one account is compromised. By centralizing password management and encouraging the use of distinct credentials for each service or application, password vaults minimize the potential fallout from security breaches and bolster overall resilience against cyber threats.

Another key feature of password vaults is their encryption capabilities. Passwords stored within these vaults are typically encrypted using robust encryption algorithms, rendering them unreadable to anyone without the corresponding decryption key. This ensures that even if unauthorized individuals gain access to the vault, the contents remain protected from prying eyes. Additionally, many password vaults offer additional security measures such as multi-factor authentication and biometric authentication, further fortifying access controls and thwarting unauthorized access attempts.

Password vaults represent a critical component of modern cybersecurity strategies, empowering individuals and organizations to manage their passwords securely and efficiently. By leveraging the advanced capabilities of password vaults, businesses can mitigate the risk of data breaches, enhance compliance with regulatory requirements, and safeguard their most sensitive assets in an increasingly interconnected world.

## Compliance and Privacy

### Why We Check

By {{< contributor author="Ben Craton" >}}

What comes to mind when you hear that a security audit is underway? Nervousness? Trepidation? It would be surprising if you found the news exhilarating, but must we associate a security audit purely with negativity?

We are all human and the fear of "being found out" is one that haunts us our whole lives. When a security audit is announced, our minds immediately go to our past failures, the mountain of work in front of us, and intimate knowledge of where we could have done better. However, the purpose of an audit is not to cast judgement or find fault, but instead to put aside that task list, discuss those mistakes, and bring weaknesses to the forefront.

In school, exams were always a stressful time. Would you pass or would you fail? The exam was the focus, and we were perhaps myopic to the real purpose of those tests: to find where your weaknesses were and train those skills further. In football, cricket, or any sport, to succeed and dominate the competition, discovering where you could improve is always the key differentiator.

Audits are a chance for us to reflect on our progress, our strategy, and our prowess. Yes, they can bring on stress and anxiety, but if we are brave enough to face the threats and competition in the market that we do on a daily basis, we can face our opportunities for growth and maturity with the same confidence.

## Security Research

By {{< contributor author="Ross Moore" >}}

We all already have tons of “security controls” in place. We have policies, procedures, and incident response plans – we just don’t call them that. When we leave the house, we do things like make sure the oven is off, close and lock doors and windows, and enable camera notifications.

For physical emergencies, we have a contact list on the fridge & in our phones; we know where the nearest hospital is; we know how to use the fire extinguisher and keep our vital documents are in a fireproof safe.

The online world is the same – it needs virtual locks, keys, plans, and backups.

Here are 3 primary activities to keep your financial information safe:

Use Strong, Unique Passwords: Use complex passwords, don’t use easily guessable information such as birthdays or names, and ensure that each of your accounts has a unique password.

Enable Two-Factor Authentication (2FA): 2FA adds an extra layer of security by requiring not only a password and username but also a piece of information that only the user can access. This greatly reduces the likelihood of unauthorized access, even if passwords are compromised.

Regularly Monitor Account Activity: Stay vigilant by regularly reviewing your account activity for unauthorized transactions. Set up alerts to notify you of any unusual activity (however you define unusual). The sooner you catch suspicious behavior, the better.
“But what if I get compromised?”

Immediately call your financial institution using the help and support number on the back of your card and talk to them. Be upfront and honest about what happened. It’s far from the first time they’ve gotten that call from customers about theft.
